A Tale of Two Innings

 

Match The First

Low Hall Farm

Season II, Chapter I

Crescent and Star the Victor.

 

 

IT WAS the best of games, it was the worst of games, it was the game of wisdom, it was the game of foolishness, it was an innings of belief, it was an innnings of incredulity, it was the bowling of White (5-0-3-14), it was the batting of Darkness (4 ducks, one goldie), it sprung from Low Hall Farm, home of nuclear winter, we had everything before us, then had nothing in the end; we were all going direct to Triumph, we ended going direct the other way - in short, the match was so far like the first season, in that our noisiest appeals insisted on being received, for edges and for leg before, but early hope fell foul eventually.

 

There were a captain with a large gut who was a bowler at a fast pace (Richardson, 3-1-3-13) on the throne of The Village; there were a batsman with a large gut undone by brisk pace (Sibson INSERT FIGURES), all alone for C&S. In both teams it was clearer than crystal that the pitch's state was not to batsmen's wishes (20 wickets for a Nelson), but the turning ball saw issues settled forever.

 

It was the year of Our Lord two thousand and five. Early jubilations were conceded to Village in the first foray, as at the year beofre. Miss Evans was recently awaiting her five-and-twentieth blessed birthday, of whom a prophetic saying ("That was fucking brilliant") had heralded the unplayable Broon (2-0-2-8, 20 balls where without willow struck not leather) and the finely fielding P.D. Wake, whose dextrous digits were made for the swallowing of Crescent and Star (63 all out).

 

Batsmen, less favoured on the whole as to bounce this variable as are their sister of the bowl and field, were felled with exceeding smoothness until Alan White's doughty turn fought strong. Under the beamers of the Crescent bowlers, he entertained the crowd, the side, with such human achievements saw victory in its sight only to have their hopes cut off: the spinners turned out with jaffas, and the Village CC bats no more could thrive, though White had knuckled down for runs with great honour (21 and game's top scorer), a succession of spin which passed into view at a distance of some twenty-two yards, and this undid the others (Village 48 all out INSERT OVERS).

 

All these things, and a thousand like them, came to pass upon the twenty-ninth of April two thousand and five. Environed by them, the Village and the Wives toiled unheeded, those two of the large guts trod with tabs on lips, The Village had grown yet it still fell. Thus the Village did conduct their Greatnesses, but the myriads of the Crescent - the Crescent as league champions - laid them to rest. Now long are the roads that lie before them.

 

Charles Dickens
