Sunshine and showers (one of which halted play). Damp pitch, but not unplayable.

(With apologies to Lord Byron)

1.

Oriel’s ground, the hallow’d place is found

In Oxford, a little way off Cowley Road

In days of yore, ‘twas Marrow’s batting ground

The maidens would coo, when out to bat he strode

Beautifully, to all parts, the ball he’d pound

And soon, the bowlers’ confidence implode

The Village, then, wished him at Bartlemas

But they were alone, bereft – Marrowless.

 

2.

Skipper Bowman walked out to the wicket

A tough choice the cap’n faced, for the pitch

Was wet and green, not for flashy cricket

To bat or bowl – he knew ‘twas a choice which

Would, if made right, be just the ticket

No head in sand: this Kiwi no ostrich.

The coin went up, and down it came; to bat

His call: we’d guts it out, and that was that.

 

3.

He walked out to open the innings with White,

Runs he foresaw – the southpaw was in form.

The first overs were cagey; the bowlers were tight

And above our heroes, a gathering storm

Which made the ball swing and dip in its flight

They battled hard; the skipper’s mind did swarm

He went for a drive, but the stroke was wild –

He’s gone! Out, departed: caught by a child.

 

4.

It meant that a prodigal son returned

Could he get our innings back on the rails?

Was the man to make the runs we yearned

Paul Collingwood lookalike Ed Hayles?

He started badly, and looked quite concerned

He took us to lunch – I’ll spare the details

Suffice to say, such sessions win matches

Edward was helped out by two dropped catches.

 

5.

Meanwhile, in the pavilion’s embrace

Another drama was unfolding due

To David Ireland who, with such good grace

Had some brand new WAGs managed to accrue

Said one Villager, “I must press my case

Upon these ladies making their debut.”

A phenomenon hard to catch in rhyme

What else to write, but that it was Lousetime?

 

6.

Said Ed, “Sod this, I’ve not batted in years

Balls to this blocking – it’s time to attack”

So after lunch to rousing Village cheers

He and Bomb went out, and gave it a thwack.

They tonked the bowling, these two cavaliers

It meant the fielders were forced to fall back.

Freed from technical penitentiaries

Both brave batsmen reached their half centuries.

 

7.

They carried on swinging – they scored and scored

But batting in this manner, it means

You live as you perish – by the sword

The oppo’s child knew his fielding routines

Thanks to his catches – advantage restored.

Out in the pavilion our team convenes

“We need 200 plus – it can’t be ignored

We need a guy to (say the men of Queens)

As Tony Greig would say –‘Come to the party’”

Step forward our Aussie – Stephen Harty.

 

8.

The antipodean made a good score

And now it fell to a Cumbrian loon

With a brand new ball, to open the door

He bowled full and straight, the Sedbergh Typhoon

We all were gripped by an esprit de corps

As Stevo made them dance to his tune.

And sure could he take his wickets no cheaper

Lo! He’s only trapped the Blues’ wicket keeper!

9.

“Come on boys, a wicket brings one more,”

Called out a fielder, as a child walked out.

The umpire, his father, began he to roar

Accused us of sledging, started to shout.

His own team mates found him a crashing bore

It needed someone to inform this lout:

“The problem, dear father competitive

Your hectoring soon grows rather repetitive.”

 

10.

Things got worse, for your bard stepped up to bowl

And didn’t want to unleash a screamer

Trying to bowl slowly, he lost all control

“Oh Christ,” all thought, “He’s let slip a beamer.”

I’d achieved the opposite of my goal

The umpire started to hiss like a steamer

The child was made of sterner stuff than his dad:

He hooked it for four – even I was glad.

 

11

Three overs left – three results on the cards

But the batsman slow-scoring, like A. Strauss

The chance of a win, he now disregards

Great bowling from Bowman, Harty and Louse.

Ireland couldn’t break through – doorknob/retards

(A Dodgeball quote), so to a public house

Awards – Stevo MOM, and now I’m a riddler.

Idiot goes to a vain diddler.
