Mostly sunny with some cloud cover towards late afternoon. And some wind, mostly from Bomb.

The Lord of the Rings

 

...being a story with mind-numbingly dull beginnings, a swashbuckling middle and a glorious ending...

 

 

The Fellowship of the Ring

 

The journey begins and Bombomir falls

 

The men of the Village gathered at their pastoral paradise for the second day of the 2009 Cambridge Tour. Sore heads were nursed; various amusing tales of the previous night recounted (most involving Louse) and for those of us who had tasted PLy’s 'stilton curry' at the India House, tales of the ring were exchanged.

 

The Rad won the toss and, perhaps hoping to cash in on the alcohol-induced frailties of the Village, put us in to bat. At first this appeared to be a reasonable tactic. The well-established partnership of Potty and Bomb sauntered out to open the Village batting and duly treated us to 10 overs that were ‘stodgier than a West Cornwall Pasty’ (it says here in the scorebook). Once again PLy was umpiring and having listened to Bomb’s complaints about his umpiring chat from the day before, duly ignored them and performed one of the most amusing pieces of physical theatre in Village history. The Aussie’s slow-mo stagger to square leg, followed by gradual collapse in to coma was a memorable moment for all spectators. The batsmen later put forward various excuses for subjecting Barton Road to this tedium:

 

1)       “It was jagging off the pitch and swinging miles”. Both culprits were later heard bemoaning the ‘disappearance’ of said jag and swing during the Rad’s innings.

Plausibility rating: low

 

2)       “I was f**king hungover, ok?” Bomb was indeed very drunk the night before.

Plausibility rating: medium

 

3)       “Look, it worked and it’s better than being 4 down after ten overs, so piss off.” Well yes it did and yes it is.

Plausibility rating: grudgingly high

 

So the two batsmen ploughed ahead un-fussed by PLy’s antics and various other rounds of sledging from the boundary. Beard, doing a more than passable impression of Gimli, was as difficult to get away as ever, while the other opening bowler kept things tight at the road end. At this point Bomb was looking in pretty good nick but then received an absolute belter of a delivery, which pitched outside off and clipped his leg stump, from Haslemere. Bomb’s dismissal saw the arrival of Youdan for his first Village innings of the season. Although Youdan played a few nice strokes, he showed signs of early season rustiness and was eventually caught off some decent bowling from Anderson.

 

 

The Two Towers

 

Helm’s deep and two hobbits journey onward

 

At this stage of our tale you might be wondering what all the fuss was about. Epic, yes, but hardly a classic. However, this was all about to change with the arrival of a Village legend at the crease.

 

The A-Rich, bearing a mighty 5lb bat hewn of shire oak, returned to his rightful place at the side of his fellow Queen. Potty’s demeanour slowly changed. Whether it was the arrival of his old brother-in-arms or the sight of Louse taking his fiancé on a ‘stroll’ round the boundary, the Scotsman’s batting spirits were fired and after seeing off the decent Rad openers, he set about taking apart the rest of the bowling attack. Think Helm’s Deep but with a bit more destruction.

 

In the meantime our captain had promoted Louse up the order to No.5 in the batting line-up. As a position where Villagers are accustomed to expect at least 34 overs of batting, the Stylist proceeded to build himself up to unprecedented levels of intensity. He declared to Marrow, “If I had a Pumpometer on my chest, it would be at the maximum.” Marrow took several steps backwards. Needless to say, the rest of the Village was getting very excited about what the over-hair-producted man was about to unleash on the Rad. Sadly Louse’s desires went unsatisfied, not for the first time that weekend. Instead we were treated to a feast of vintage Village batting as Potty pulled ball after ball through midwicket and A-Rich delivered some classic lofted drives over mid-off. 

 

With 5 overs to go it looked like Potty could post his first Village century, but the combination of a tight spell from the Rad leftie, Butcher, and A-Rich’s own merciless charge towards a fifty meant that he was limited to a very impressive 85 not out. This was a fine and fitting way to leave the Village for his early summer marriage break. At the end of his 35 over journey, Potty collapsed over the edge of the boundary under the concerned eyes of his big-boned companion.

 

The innings ended on 166 for two.

 

The Return of the King

 

Many battles occur; the king returns; the crack of doom.

 

The Village bowling was once again opened by PLy, who sent down missile after missile in a truly terrifying spell of bowling. The batsmen swished and swashed to no great effect for 3 overs, but without any reward for the bowler, and so PLy gave his captain the proverbial ‘f**k this’ and took himself out of the attack. At the other end, KP was bowling some extremely tight stuff and eventually managed to treat one of the openers, Wilson, to a visit from Professor Plum of Lower Barton Way. 

 

After PLy’s efforts the Cobra was unleashed but it would not be this King’s day, as he came up against the Rad’s No.3, Dawson, whose leg side game was perhaps even more accomplished than Potty’s. One of these lusty blows through midwicket was almost snaffled by said Village opener (and it would have been a blinder) but it popped out of his hand and the batsman would go on to make a very useful 40.

 

By now, the pent up tension of the weekend was threatening to get on top of Louse and so, for everyone’s sake, the captain replaced himself with the Village’s inswinging tyro. The bowler had already become more than a little frustrated at a few missed opportunities off his bowling and so Marrow was disappointed to shell an uppish cover drive (though, like Potty’s, it was approaching Mach 4) from Lewis, who had now batted himself in to some form. Urged on by his teammates, the Louse began to treat the batsman to some of his special stares and, in the sixth over of his spell, made the breakthrough with Marrow catching Lewis at mid-off.

 

In a day of high drama, no one was really ready for what happened next.

 

Eddy Francis started to mark out his run up.

 

“Christ has risen!” cried Page

 

But this time He would be doing the crucifying. For it was to be the returning Messiah who made the biggest mark on this game. The Village’s Spin Bowler du Jour produced a sumptuous spell accounting for five of the Rad’s batsmen (including the key wicket of Dawson), thereby securing a permanent place on Barton Road’s honours board, were one to exist. One by one the Rad batsmen stepped up to the crease before being sent back to the pavilion by deliveries that made Warne’s to Gatting look like an arm ball. The extent to which Eddy had the opposition tied in knots is best exhibited by the fact that 4 of the wickets were clean bowled.

 

Parp! continued his early season good form behind the stumps by conceding very few byes and therefore building pressure on the batsmen through their innings. It seems that negative betting can indeed produce excellent performances. Ahem.

 

The only controversial moment of the day came at the other end where Marrow sent down a spitting, 95 mph bouncer (or top-edged full toss, depending on whom you want to believe) in to Page’s chin, which immediately split open and drenched the wicket in blood. The stoic and not-in-any-way-inebriated Page attempted to carry on but various doctoring Wagahos sent him off to hospital to get the wound stitched up. Mathew had threatened a bit of Rad resistance before scooping one of Marrow’s balls to cover where Youdan gratefully accepted the catch. Haslemere was then bowled and the Rad finished on a creditable 115.

 

With that, the 2009 Cambridge Tour ended in happiness and success, and the Villagers departed for lands afar…

 

 

Man of the Match

 

Potty’s innings was magnificent and set the foundations for victory but the award has to go to Eddy for joining the Village bowling greats by producing a very special Michelle.

 

Honourable mentions go to David Ireland for sticking around to provide strong boundary chat, despite ruling himself out of selection, and to the WAGs for their excellent support all weekend.

 

 

Village Idiot

 

A depressing shortage of idiots in this match so we’ll give it to PLy for his fine moment of comedy.
