Grey.  Light rain, later.

Anyone who has read my ludicrously titled and falsely self-effacing cricket blog (crapcricketer.blogspot.co.uk) cannot have failed but to realise that I am an all-rounder of extraordinary natural talent.

As a boy I played with, and imparted my experience to, the current England wicketkeeper-batsmen Matt Prior.  I’ve played County Cricket (Hampshire Youth), League cricket, Village cricket and Joke cricket, the latter with celebrity team The R*****n, who manage to lose regularly: even with me in the team!

But I didn’t get where I am today by waffling, so on to the match report for this seasons game against Agricola CC at Parliament Hill, North London.   As a classically educated, Oxbridge graduate (Brooks College, Oxford), the first thing I would like to observe is that there seems to be a degree of inconsistency amongst the Agricola players – who are a very friendly bunch – about the pronunciation of their team’s name.  This seems to be down to more than the slight Suth Effrican lilt that one or two of them possess: half cheer “come on ah-GRIK-uh-luh”, the correct pronunciation of Roman general Gnaeus Julius Agricola’s cognomen, while half shout “good stuff an-gri-KOLA” which I assume to be either a little known but aggression inducing product of the Coca-Cola Company or something to do with the clubs early history, as a Ministry for Agriculture works team.

Anyway Agricola, however it is pronounced, won the toss on a very green wicket with a Lords-like slope and elected to field.  The steel-grey sky promised rain.

I didn’t get where I am today without knowing a real winner when I see one, and I was delighted to see Bomb-protégée Matthew Boa stride out to open the batting. Boa is a bowling all-rounder, with a control of line-and-length that is almost as good as my own, and a delightful short-but-curved run which would have been perfect for the backyard Test matches I played as a boy.  As a batsmen, he is solid with a short back lift that enables resolute defense and Bombesque fast hands that enable him to send the ball crashing home when he so chooses!

Aided by some fielding errors by the MAFF-men, Boa hit a career best 45, including four fours and two sixes – one a gorgeous leg glance which carried the boundary on the full and landed near the Village WAGs.

Other than he and Nick Troja, all the Village batmen struggled and failed against some accurate Agricola bowling on a very slow wicket which offered some inconsistent bounce.  

Batting at number 6, Troja hit a tidy 51, playing the ball very late so as to be able to adjust to the above mentioned problematic bounce.   He was well supported by Craig Woodhouse during a late 5-over partnership for the 9th wicket.

I didn’t get where I am today without recognising a good score and our total of 156 all out from 39.1 overs was probably 20 short of that, but with a strong bowling attack and a wicket that would assist we remained confident.

There was a slight drizzle and live music coming from the Parliament Hill Bandstand by the time we took the field.  Due to space constraints and a need to respond urgently to some Mav-baits on VCC Chat, I will not detail my considered thoughts on the Salsa band here.  Should you be interested please visit my stream-of-consciousness Twitter feed where I very regularly expurgate my sophisticated opinions on Music, Politics and Johann Hari.

After losing a couple of early wickets, Agricola put together a strong partnership for the 3rd wicket reaching 60-2 prior to the introduction our hero Boa one over before drinks.  Boa stuck immediately, drawing aggressive number 4 Lillo into a mistimed drive to Kris Bird at mid-off. 

The Boa Constrictor then went onto a roll, keeping things very tight and grabbing wickets seemingly at will.  In an 8 over spell, he picked up 6 wickets for only 14 runs: another career best, Boa’s second of the day.  A team member observed afterwards that while Boa had enjoyed some luck during his innings, the bowling figures were fully deserved.  A highlight for me was a sharp caught and bowled, diving away to his right.  It was a catch that I would have been proud of!

I didn’t get where I am today without knowing a slight wobble when I see one, and this was a big one from Agricola: they lost 7 wickets for 30 runs.

Neither did I get where I am today without recognising a rib-tickling play on words when I hear one, and the least said about M’Harty’s shout from midwicket that “it’s raining but the runs seem to have stopped” midway through Matthew Boa’s spell the better.

Adam Collins finished things off with a fiery couple of overs at the Agricola tail which were interestingly about 20% quicker than his early spell against the opening bats.  He’s clearly been talking to Paul Bowman.

Agricola were bowled out for 122 in 34.2 overs.  We retired to The Bull & Last to discuss WAGs and watch a local jock make a complete mess of an internet date.  Spies report that at the end of the evening he leaned in for a kiss and received a hand shake.
