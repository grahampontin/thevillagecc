The Village Take On The Rampant Badgers or What To Write When You’ve Forgotten What Happened In The Game

The day before Bastille Day marked a key point in VCC Season 2013. After two losses on the trot, both [probably] sustained under the stewardship of captain Eklavya, whispers of doubt had begun to be heard in some quarters on the subject of our Indian talisman’s leadership.  Among the whisperers, none was less vocal than stand-in captain and aspiring Club President and CEO, Matthew “The Coming Man” Boa. Would a return to Tooting and the ground we possibly first used in 2012 see the masses appeased with fireworks or the guillotine? Who would culled? The Badgers? Or the VCC’s complacent elite?

Deciding where to begin recording the action in a VCC match is frequently not as straightforward as you might imagine. In an ideal world, the obvious starting point would be the moment at which the captains toss, followed by the inevitable brisk hand-shake, a return to the sidelines, a chorus of moans from one of the teams and the same team’s subsequent passage out on to the field in hastily-donned whites. Somehow, it’s rarely that straightforward for the Village. Certain unnamed players can be relied upon to be a minimum of half an hour late. Even when everyone appears to have arrived, it’s far from a given that both teams have 11. In this case, in addition to the usual VCC suspects being a little late, the badgers were clearly far from a full sett. Despite being laid up with a torn ACL, Captain Tom (?), heroically, had shown great willing, limping along to the ground to corral his men. The same could not be said of many of his co-Badgers. Even once we’d agreed that evergreen VCC all-rounder Bn would be leased to the oppo,  it still took some time, and the loan of a fielder in the form of the ever-willing Will Hayles (? and later Alex Odlum?) for the oppo to have a plausible quorum. Tant pis. After the start, the Badgers’ numbers were boosted by sporadic arrivals during the course of the day, notably by one player who called in at the allotted start time to report that he was already on Tooting Common looking for the game, but who, bizarrely, took several more hours to materialise.

Harsh, perhaps: as both teams’ nearly-XIs took to the field, it became clear to newcomers (including your author) that Tooting Bec Common is in fact a vast space, big enough to conceal any number of cricket matches; hot, browning grassland punctuated by a few small copses (one of which had the makings of – and indeed became – a makeshift latrine) none of which would do much to shield the players from a brutal July sun.  Nor was there much breeze as Alan “A-Bomb” White and Alex Odlum took the crease. On the face of it, Odlum looks a fine bat, but one who has yet to find traction for Village. More of the same for him that day as another promising start was cut off early by the always-fiery bowling of always-fiery Hampton.

Morgans at number three seemed to have more staying power and built a decent partnership with White, who looked in belligerent form. I have no idea at all at what point they variously got out, but it seems likely that Bomb anchored the innings before opening up and holing out having played some thunderous strokes for boundaries, including a ‘rare’ six.

Your author does remember some controversy surrounding the innings of Nas. The owner of the Village’s pornest car did not walk late in the innings, when he had seemingly quite palpably nicked it behind, claiming afterwards to have a ‘very poor short term memory’. Nas went on to score a good thirty more runs. The oppo displayed rather more honesty during their innings, walking when challenged for various nicks behind, possibly off, as luck would have it, the bowling of Nas. To say that the match turned on the incident might be over-egging it, but the runs scored by Nas (and not scored by the honest, walking Badgers, would certainly have enough to have change the result of the match, which is slightly different but not insignificant.

Otherwise the match was played out in customary village form. Ireland came in at 5 and was out for 0 off the bowling of Magnus (who took two the badgers’ 5 wickets) with a straight one. Perhaps next season, DI will learn to defend the first few and thus learn to compile 8-ball ducks instead of two-ball ones. Hayles put on some well-hit with three fours before falling to Andre and Nas and Boa were there at the end of the innings with a combined 43 taking the VCC to 193/5.

Would it be enough? Probably.

Catches from Hayles and Nick Thompson did for the two opening Badgers, the latter, Brooker, offering more resistance. Overall, one can probably read the extent of the Badgers’ un-vigilance in the fact that all but two of their out batsmen were out caught. Somehow between batsmen four and five (Peter, Andre) the Badgers seemed to cross their river Jordan and found a whole new world of form. Pete never really had the chance to give Village a flava of his batting, holing out to Nas off the bowling of Wildman. But following Peter’s dismissal, Andre proceeded to Accelerate,  which gimme little sign that behind closed doors he might have a whole new world of class. The Village bowlers certainly tried to turn it up, but with Eddy Francis in abnormally generous form, certainty of Andre’s scoring shots began to look unconditional. While Boa and White took the wickets of the two Johns, Andre started to notch up runs at a rate that was mad. But if Andre’s run scoring was insane, his ultimate dismissal was insania. Perhaps distracted by a mysterious girl (Fran Bell) on the boundary, he absently called for a second run. Thompson’s express throw from mid-wicket made sure it wasn’t a perfect night for Andre, who departed run out on 73, probably leaving Hampton and Tim to try to bat out a lot of runs in not much time. The long road back proved too long and when non-batsman Tim was bowled by Nas, with no one left to replace him, Village, trying to play the game the right way, ungenerously allowed the same batsman to return to the crease, only to be bowled again, in identical fashion, by Nas, the next ball.

So basically, we won, ungloriously, as is sometimes our wont.

Afterwards, if it was this match, we all went for a curry in the upstairs room of a not-that-local establishment recommended by one of the Badgers. Mav drove Bomb, Marow and a few others through pouring rain to a curry house an improbable distance away from Tooting Bec. The utility of the ride didn’t prevent Mav’s passengers from giving their driver a hilarious ribbing for the fact he was driving an ‘uncle’s car’ – specifically a 1990s era E-class Mercedes. (For the record, this model is one of the most reliable Mercedes have ever produced, so for those who thrive on this sort of thing you have a good forty years of ridiculing left.)

 

In fact, I’m talking b@lls. The Badgers match was on that gloriously hot day when Morgs, Hayles and Mav (and possibly Eddy?) elected to walk 800 yards across the common, past a game of football being played only by Somalians, to the magnificent Tooting Lido, which I encourage you to visit. At 94? Metres long, it’s one of the largest swimming pools in Europe and was such a perfect way to cool down after a hot day in the field, it made your author wonder why we don’t always play near public baths. Half an hour later, blessing an archetypal Victorian public spirit and engineering that has withstood 200 years of South London onslaught, three refreshed Villagers rejoined their team mates at that pub opposite the Sainsburys, where they celebrated the day’s mildly unsporting victory.
